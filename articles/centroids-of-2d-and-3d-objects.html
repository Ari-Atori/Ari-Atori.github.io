CALCULATING THE CENTROID OF 2D AND 3D OBJECTS

When performing physics simulations, one of the more crucial operations of your engine is ensuring an object tumbles in the correct direction and orientation. Part of the process is calculating if the center of mass (or centroid) is within an object's area of stability. Otherwise, calculate which edge or point to rotate off of.


This article is about computing this centroid, or the average position of all the material constituting your object. The algorithm for calculating the centroid will depend on whether the object is 2D or 3D, as both of the presented algorithms will assume uniform-density bodies with non-zero area or volume. All of these formulas will take linear time and constant space.

CALCULATING 3D CENTROIDS

3D is where it gets slightly more complicated. As with the 2D case, we could break down our shape into the list of triangles provided, whose centroids were easily calculable. Here, this gets more complicated, as the triangle list used to constitute models or hitboxes is merely the surface of the 3D model. We could try to turn the polyhedra into simpler shapes, like the tetrahedron. However, there is a class of shapes that are impossible to tetrahedralize without adding more vertices along the edges or faces.


Here's what we can do instead: Take each triangle, draw sides from each edge, straight down until it touches the XZ plane. This operation will produce a wonky, irregular triangular prism with a flat triangular base.

We'll do something based on the shoelace formula. If the triangle faces upwards, the volume below would be part of the polyhedron. Thus, we add it to the total volume. The same logic can apply similarly if the triangle is facing downward. Because that may designate the face as a bottom face, and the volume covered is not part of the volume. Therefore, we need to subtract from the total volume. We can do this for every single triangle in our closed polyhedra, getting our volume for our polyhedra. This formula will work with non-convex polyhedra even.

CONCLUSION

Those two algorithms above are the formulas for the generic polygons and polyhedrons that make up most models in video games. While you can reduce the formula for simpler shapes like spheres, axis-aligned bounding boxes, and capsules, this page should give you the tools to compute irregular shapes in need of extra precision.
